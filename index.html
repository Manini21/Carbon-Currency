01
Carbon 101

What exactly is
a carbon credit?

A carbon credit represents the removal or reduction of 1 metric ton of CO₂ from the atmosphere.

It is a tradable certificate — issued, tracked, and exchanged on regulated and voluntary markets worldwide.

If a company emits CO₂, it can purchase carbon credits to "offset" those emissions — theoretically balancing what it releases into the atmosphere.

04
The Reality Check

Credits issued.
But did emissions fall?

Independent research found that up to 80% of forest offset credits reviewed in 2024 had no real climate benefit. Meanwhile global emissions hit a new record. The numbers don't add up.

● Forest Carbon Sink
−2.4 Gt
CO₂ absorbed by offset forests (claimed, 2024)
Projects sell credits claiming forests will absorb millions of tons over decades. A 2024 Berkeley study found average over-crediting of 6.5x across REDD+ projects.
● Industrial Emissions
+37.4 Gt
Global CO₂ emissions (2024, record high)
Companies purchased credits and continued business as usual. 2024 marked the first year global average temperature exceeded 1.5°C above pre-industrial levels — the Paris Agreement's red line.

06
Analytical Perspective

Market Signal or
Structural Change?

The four findings below draw directly from the longitudinal emissions data (1995–2024). Each finding compares observed country trajectories against policy context — carbon pricing, energy transitions, and economic structure — to assess what the data can and cannot tell us about causation.

EU countries show 30–45% reductions since 2005.
Germany (−34.06%), France (−36.55%), and the United Kingdom (−45.14%) all show substantial declines. The EU ETS, combined with renewable energy mandates and efficiency standards, correlates with this trend — though isolating the pricing signal from broader policy packages is methodologically complex.
China and India emissions increased significantly.
China's emissions rose +108.93% since 2005, India's by +167.15%. Both economies underwent rapid industrialisation and electrification during this period. China launched a national ETS in 2021 — its effect on the trajectory will take years to assess.
The U.S. shows a moderate decline of −19.96%.
The United States has no federal carbon price. Reductions are attributed primarily to the shale gas boom displacing coal in power generation, efficiency gains, and state-level programs (California, RGGI). The absence of national carbon pricing makes this case analytically distinct.
Correlation is visible. Causation is contested.
No peer-reviewed consensus exists that carbon markets alone drove emission reductions in any single economy. The evidence suggests they may be a contributing factor within a broader policy framework — alongside energy standards, technology deployment, and economic cycles.
